===============================================================================
CORE META-RULES CMR-03 - DOCUMENT CREATION AND CHANGE RULES
===============================================================================

Status:
-------
FROZEN

Description:
------------
Applies to all specification documents and their evolution.
Changes here have cascading impact and must be rare and explicit.

Purpose:
--------
This document defines how Turnix specification documents are created, edited,
reviewed, and evolved over time.

It defines:
- LOCKED / OPEN / IMPLEMENTATION markers
- rules for preserving meaning and preventing silent semantic drift
- rules for full-document updates and safe reorganization
- constraints on examples and non-normative content
- the structure used for normative rule prose

It does NOT define:
- physical formatting rules (see CMR-01)
- document title banner rules (see CMR-02)
- indexing rules and conflict resolution (see CMR-04)
- cross-thread reference and retrieval rules (see CMR-05)

Upstream Documents:
------------------
- CMR-01
- CMR-02
- CMR-04
- CMR-05


===============================================================================
01. NORMATIVE LANGUAGE
===============================================================================

01.1. Turnix specifications express normative meaning through explicit,
      deterministic prose rather than through RFC-style keywords.

01.2. Normative meaning is expressed through clear actor-action statements
      that describe system behavior, guarantees, or constraints.

01.3. Normative rules remain understandable without relying on special
      vocabulary markers.

01.4. RFC-style requirement keywords (for example: MUST, SHOULD, MAY) carry
      no special meaning in Turnix specifications.

01.5. The tokens "SHOULD" and "SHOULD NOT" are forbidden in authoritative
      documents because they introduce ambiguous advisory semantics.

01.6. When such tokens appear in draft text, the author removes or rewrites
      them before the document is treated as authoritative.

01.7. Normative meaning derives from sentence structure and document context,
      not from reserved keywords.


===============================================================================
02. DOCUMENT INTENDED USE
===============================================================================

02.1. Turnix specification documents serve as contracts and reference
      material for both humans and Large Language Models.

02.1.1. In Turnix documents, "language model assistant" means a
        language model acting in an assistant role, including
        LLM-based systems.

02.2. Documents remain suitable for use by LLMs for:
      - technical discussion
      - drafting and revising other specification documents
      - assisting with implementation decisions
      - guiding local or remote coding LLM models

02.3. Documents remain readable and understandable by humans without
      requiring tooling, rendering, or preprocessing.

02.4. Documents do not rely on implicit context, conversational history, or
      unstated assumptions for correctness.


===============================================================================
03. DOCUMENT STATES AND MARKERS
===============================================================================

03.1. Documents and document content may use the following state markers:

      - LOCKED
      - OPEN
      - IMPLEMENTATION

03.2. These markers describe the completion state, stability, and
      intent of the content they apply to.

03.3. The presence of document state markers does not change:
      - the document DOC_TIER
      - the document DOC_ID
      - the Canonical Index DOC_AUTHORITATIVE_STATUS

03.4. Marker labels are not normative keywords.
      Marker labels do not create obligations by themselves.

03.5. Marker syntax uses one of the following forms:

      A) Block label form:
         The marker appears as the first non-space text on a line, followed
         by ":".

         Examples:
         LOCKED:
         OPEN:
         IMPLEMENTATION:

      B) Inline label form:
         The marker appears in parentheses as part of an inline label.

         Examples:
         Span identity (LOCKED):
         Storage format (OPEN):

03.6. Marker labels scope to the content that immediately follows them
      within the containing section until one of the following occurs:
      - another marker label appears
      - the section ends

03.7. Marker labels do not appear in main section titles.


===============================================================================
04. LOCKED
===============================================================================

04.1. LOCKED marks content that is considered finalized.

04.2. LOCKED content does not change in meaning.

04.3. LOCKED content does not weaken guarantees, contracts, or invariants.

04.4. LOCKED content does not introduce ambiguity.

04.5. LOCKED content may be clarified or disambiguated only when all of the
      following conditions are true:
      - the original meaning remains preserved
      - the clarification introduces no new behavior
      - the clarification does not weaken or expand guarantees

04.6. LOCKED content remains preserved in the document unless an explicit
      and documented decision supersedes or deprecates it.


===============================================================================
05. OPEN
===============================================================================

05.1. OPEN marks content that is intentionally unfinished, undecided, or
      incomplete.

05.2. OPEN content exists to:
      - track unresolved questions
      - prevent accidental omission of topics
      - document known ambiguity

05.3. OPEN content may be freely changed, reordered, expanded,
      resolved, or removed.

05.4. OPEN content does not contradict LOCKED content.

05.5. OPEN content does not imply guarantees or contracts that are not
      explicitly declared as LOCKED.


===============================================================================
06. IMPLEMENTATION
===============================================================================

06.1. IMPLEMENTATION marks content that is intentionally left as an
      implementation detail.

06.2. IMPLEMENTATION content obeys all LOCKED constraints.

06.3. IMPLEMENTATION content does not introduce new authority semantics.

06.4. IMPLEMENTATION content may evolve without changing system guarantees.

06.5. IMPLEMENTATION content is not treated as normative behavior outside
      its explicit scope.


===============================================================================
07. STRUCTURING DOCUMENT CONTENT
===============================================================================

07.1. Related concepts are defined in a single coherent section
      whenever possible.

07.2. Separate sections are used only when:
      - topics intersect but are not hierarchically related
      - isolation is required to avoid ambiguity
      - cross-references would otherwise become unclear

07.3. Section numbering and ordering may change.

07.4. All affected references are updated when reordering or renumbering
      occurs.


===============================================================================
08. CHANGING EXISTING DOCUMENTS
===============================================================================

08.1. When a document changes, the updated document is produced as a
      complete, whole document.

08.2. Changes do not introduce silent semantic drift.

08.3. Existing meaning is not lost as a side effect of reorganization,
      rewording, or deduplication.

08.4. Content is not omitted unless the omission is intentional and the
      removed meaning is explicitly addressed.

08.5. Reordering, renumbering, splitting, merging, and deduplication of
      sections is allowed.

08.6. If content remains unchanged in meaning, it still appears in the
      updated document.


===============================================================================
09. EXAMPLES AND NON-NORMATIVE CONTENT
===============================================================================

09.1. Examples are non-normative.

09.2. Examples do not:
      - introduce new behavior
      - introduce side effects
      - imply guarantees
      - contradict LOCKED content

09.3. Examples are written so that removing them does not change document
      meaning.


===============================================================================
10. IMPLICIT OPEN ITEMS
===============================================================================

10.1. When implicit but unspecified topics can be reasonably deduced from
      document context, the author may add those topics to the document.

10.2. Such additions are marked as OPEN.

10.3. Implicit OPEN additions exist to improve completeness, not to force
      decisions.


===============================================================================
11. AUTHORING, REVIEW, AND ACCEPTANCE
===============================================================================

11.1. Turnix document workflow distinguishes three roles:
      - drafting author
      - reviewer
      - acceptance authority

11.2. The drafting author produces or edits document text.

11.3. The drafting author may be:
      - a human author
      - a language model assistant operating under user instruction

11.3.1. The drafting author may revise any part of the document,
        including full-document restructuring, provided the document
        change rules and LOCKED constraints remain satisfied.

11.4. The reviewer evaluates proposed text for correctness, internal
      consistency, and alignment with upstream and downstream documents.

11.5. The acceptance authority decides whether proposed text is accepted,
      rejected, reverted, or revised further.

11.6. Draft authorship does not grant authority to make a document
      authoritative.

11.7. A document becomes authoritative only after explicit acceptance by
      the responsible human authority.

11.8. A language model assistant does not independently determine
      obsolescence, validity, or supersession unless explicitly instructed
      by the human authority.

11.9. This section does not override requirements in CMR-05 related to
      missing references and clarification.

11.10. When obsolescence occurs, the document explicitly states it.


===============================================================================
12. VERSION HYGIENE AND RETRIEVAL
===============================================================================

12.1. Documents may maintain a single current authoritative revision.

12.2. Upstream and downstream documents may be updated together to reflect
      changes.

12.3. When a document includes Upstream Documents and/or Downstream
      Documents blocks (CMR-02), those blocks may guide coordinated review.

12.3.1. These blocks are not treated as authority semantics.

12.4. Draft handling and doc-meta behavior follow CMR-01.
      Draft working copies may omit doc-meta when CMR-01 allows it.


===============================================================================
13. FORMAT COMPLIANCE AND LINTING BEHAVIOR
===============================================================================

13.1. When producing a complete document output, a language model assistant
      attempts to follow the physical formatting rules defined in CMR-01 as a
      best-effort during generation.

13.1.1. The assistant does not perform post-output formatting audits unless
        explicitly instructed by the user.

13.2. A language model assistant does not function as a formatting
      linter except where section 13.4 permits it.

13.2.1. When the user did not explicitly request a formatting check,
        the assistant does not proactively audit formatting or report
        formatting violations.

13.3. Formatting rules exist to improve readability and anchor stability.
      Minor formatting violations do not represent semantic changes.

13.3.1. Structural format violations that break section boundaries (for
        example: whitespace-only lines where blank lines are required) are
        not considered minor.

13.4. When the user explicitly requests a formatting check, the assistant
      may point out formatting issues relevant to the requested task.

13.5. When a formatting issue prevents correct document structure
      (for example: missing banner, missing end marker, or ambiguous
      section boundaries), the assistant requests clarification or
      produces a corrected draft.

13.6. The "not a formatting linter" constraint applies only when the user
      did not explicitly request a formatting check.

13.6.1. When the user explicitly requests a formatting check, the assistant
        may audit formatting and report violations within the scope
        requested.

13.7. The assistant enforces or checks CMR-01 formatting only to the extent
      required to satisfy the user's explicit request.


===============================================================================
14. RULE STRUCTURE
===============================================================================

14.1. Each normative sentence defines exactly one rule.

14.2. A rule identifies three elements:
      - actor
      - action
      - condition (optional)

14.3. The actor remains explicit. Responsibility is not implied.

14.4. When a rule depends on conditions, the conditions appear explicitly.

14.5. Logical conjunctions do not hide multiple conditions or behaviors
      in a single sentence.

14.6. A sentence does not define multiple independent behaviors.

14.7. When behavior remains intentionally open, the document states both:
      - what may vary
      - which invariant remains preserved.

14.8. Normative rules remain clear without relying on special keywords.

14.9. When removing RFC-style keywords does not change the meaning of a
      sentence, the rule structure is considered correct.


===============================================================================
15. CANONICAL SEMANTIC TOKENS
===============================================================================

15.1. Certain phrases act as canonical semantic tokens across Turnix
      specification documents.

15.2. Canonical semantic tokens represent shared concepts used across
      multiple documents. These tokens function as stable semantic anchors
      that preserve cross-document meaning.

15.3. Canonical semantic tokens remain textually stable across documents
      unless an explicit coordinated revision occurs.

15.4. When an author alters or removes a canonical semantic token,
      the change is treated as a semantic change and follows the
      document change discipline defined in this document.

15.5. Canonical semantic tokens commonly include:
      - Canonical Index
      - DOC_ID
      - DOC_TIER
      - Canonical Index DOC_AUTHORITATIVE_STATUS
      - LOCKED
      - OPEN
      - IMPLEMENTATION

15.6. The list above is not exhaustive. Additional tokens may become
      canonical through consistent usage across the specification corpus.

15.7. Authors do not replace canonical semantic tokens with synonyms,
      paraphrases, or stylistic variations when such changes would weaken
      cross-document traceability.

15.8. Surrounding prose may be clarified provided the canonical semantic
      token itself remains unchanged.

15.9. This rule prevents silent semantic drift caused by wording changes
      that break shared terminology across documents.
